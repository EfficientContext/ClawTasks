# American Alligator (*Alligator mississippiensis*)

The American alligator (*Alligator mississippiensis*) is a large crocodilian native to the southeastern United States. A member of the family Alligatoridae, it is one of only two extant species in the genus *Alligator*, the other being the Chinese alligator (*A. sinensis*). The American alligator is renowned as a keystone species and ecosystem engineer in freshwater wetland habitats, and its recovery from near-extinction in the twentieth century stands as one of the earliest and most celebrated success stories of the Endangered Species Act.

## Description and Range

Adult male American alligators typically measure 3.4 to 4.6 meters in length and weigh between 200 and 450 kilograms, though exceptional individuals may exceed 4.5 meters. Females are considerably smaller, usually reaching 2.6 to 3 meters. The body is broad and robust, with a wide, U-shaped snout that distinguishes alligators from the more V-shaped snouts of true crocodiles. Coloration in adults is dark grey to black on the dorsal surface, with a lighter, cream-colored underside. Juveniles display bright yellow crossbands on a black background, which fade with age.

The American alligator ranges across the southeastern United States, from the coastal plain of North Carolina southward through South Carolina, Georgia, and throughout Florida, then westward along the Gulf Coast through Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, and into eastern Texas. Smaller populations exist in Arkansas and isolated areas of Oklahoma. The species is most abundant in Louisiana and Florida, which together account for the majority of the wild population, estimated at approximately 5 million individuals. Louisiana alone harbors roughly 2 million alligators in its extensive coastal marshes, swamps, and bayous.

Unlike the saltwater crocodile, the American alligator has limited salt tolerance. While it possesses lingual salt glands, these are non-functional, restricting the species primarily to freshwater habitats including swamps, marshes, rivers, ponds, and lakes. In southern Florida, the American alligator coexists with the American crocodile (*Crocodylus acutus*), which preferentially inhabits saltwater and brackish environments. The two species can be distinguished by snout shape and coloration, with the alligator's broader U-shaped snout and darker coloration contrasting with the crocodile's narrower snout and lighter olive-grey color.

## Habitat Engineering

The American alligator is widely recognized as an ecosystem engineer, a species that physically modifies its environment in ways that create habitat for other organisms. The most notable example of this behavior is the construction of "alligator holes." During the dry season, alligators excavate and maintain depressions in marshes and wetlands using their bodies, tails, and claws. These depressions, which can measure several meters across and a meter or more in depth, retain water long after surrounding areas have dried out.

Alligator holes serve as critical dry-season refugia for a diverse community of aquatic and semi-aquatic organisms, including fish, turtles, snakes, frogs, wading birds, and aquatic invertebrates. During droughts, these depressions become the only available aquatic habitat over large areas, concentrating wildlife and sustaining populations that would otherwise perish. When rains return, the organisms sheltered in alligator holes disperse and recolonize the broader landscape, functioning as source populations for recovery.

This habitat engineering role makes the American alligator a keystone species in southeastern wetlands, particularly in the Florida Everglades. The removal of alligators from an ecosystem can trigger cascading ecological effects, including the loss of dry-season refugia and shifts in prey species abundance. Alligator nests also contribute to habitat diversity; the large mounds of decomposing vegetation create elevated sites for trees and shrubs in otherwise flat, flooded landscapes.

## ESA Recovery

The American alligator's recovery under the Endangered Species Act is one of the most cited examples of successful wildlife conservation legislation in the United States. By the mid-twentieth century, decades of unregulated hunting for hides and habitat loss had reduced alligator populations to dangerously low levels across much of the species' range. The species was listed as endangered in 1967 under the Endangered Species Preservation Act, the precursor to the modern ESA.

Following federal protection, combined with state-level management programs and the prohibition of commercial hunting, alligator populations recovered steadily. By 1987, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service reclassified the American alligator to "threatened due to similarity of appearance," a designation intended to prevent confusion with endangered crocodilian species rather than because the alligator itself remained at risk. The population had rebounded to several million individuals.

Today, regulated hunting seasons are permitted in multiple states, including Louisiana, Florida, Texas, Georgia, and South Carolina. Louisiana operates both a wild harvest program and a highly successful alligator farming industry. Alligator farming, which involves the collection of eggs from wild nests and subsequent rearing of hatchlings in controlled environments, generates significant economic revenue while maintaining wild population levels. The combined economic value of the American alligator through farming, regulated hunting, ecotourism, and related industries amounts to hundreds of millions of dollars annually.

## Ecology and Diet

The American alligator is a generalist predator whose diet shifts with body size. Hatchlings and juveniles feed on insects, snails, small fish, and tadpoles. As alligators grow, they incorporate increasingly larger prey, including larger fish, turtles, snakes, wading birds, raccoons, muskrats, and nutria. Large adults are capable of taking white-tailed deer and feral hogs, particularly when these animals enter the water. Cannibalism occurs, with large adults occasionally consuming smaller alligators.

Alligators play an important role in controlling populations of certain species. In some areas, they are significant predators of invasive nutria and feral hogs, both of which cause extensive damage to wetland habitats. The alligator's consumption of gar and other large fish helps regulate fish communities, while its predation on raccoons can indirectly benefit nesting sea turtles and ground-nesting birds by suppressing nest predator populations.

The species exhibits notable cold tolerance compared to most crocodilians. During winter months, American alligators enter a dormant state known as brumation, reducing metabolic activity and becoming largely inactive. In the event of a sudden freeze, alligators have been observed engaging in a remarkable survival behavior: they position their snouts above the water surface as ice forms, allowing the tip of the snout with the nostrils to remain exposed above the ice layer. This behavior permits continued breathing even when the body is encased in ice, and alligators can survive in this state for extended periods until temperatures rise again.

## Human Coexistence

The expansion of human development into alligator habitat across the southeastern United States has increased the frequency of human-alligator interactions. In Florida alone, the state wildlife agency receives approximately 15,000 to 20,000 alligator-related complaints annually. A statewide Nuisance Alligator Program employs licensed trappers to remove alligators deemed a threat to human safety. Despite the large population and frequent encounters, fatal attacks remain relatively rare, averaging approximately one death per year in Florida.

Public education programs emphasize the importance of not feeding wild alligators, as habituated animals lose their natural wariness of humans. Laws in most states within the alligator's range prohibit feeding wild alligators.

The American alligator holds considerable cultural significance in the southeastern United States. It serves as the state reptile of Florida, Louisiana, and Mississippi. Its presence is a major draw for ecotourism, particularly in the Everglades and Louisiana bayou country, where guided airboat tours and alligator-viewing excursions represent a substantial component of the local tourism economy.
