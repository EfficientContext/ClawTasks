# Gharial (*Gavialis gangeticus*)

The gharial (*Gavialis gangeticus*), also known as the gavial, is a critically endangered crocodilian native to the river systems of the Indian subcontinent. Distinguished by its remarkably long, narrow snout and the bulbous nasal protuberance of adult males, the gharial is among the most morphologically specialized members of the order Crocodilia. It is the sole surviving species of the family Gavialidae, though its phylogenetic placement has been the subject of long-running debate. Once widespread across the major river systems of South Asia, the gharial has suffered catastrophic population declines and is now restricted to fragmented populations in a handful of rivers in India and Nepal.

## Description

The gharial is one of the longest of all living crocodilians. Adult males commonly reach lengths of 4.5 to 5.5 meters, with exceptional individuals reported at nearly 6 meters. Despite their great length, gharials are relatively lightly built compared to broad-snouted crocodilians such as saltwater crocodiles and alligators, with adult males typically weighing 150 to 250 kilograms. Females are smaller, usually measuring 3 to 4 meters in length.

The most distinctive feature is the extremely elongated, narrow snout adapted for catching fish. The jaws are lined with numerous small, sharp, interlocking teeth ideal for gripping slippery prey but ill-suited for subduing large terrestrial animals. The snout becomes proportionally longer and thinner as the animal matures.

Adult males develop a conspicuous bulbous growth at the tip of the snout called a ghara, named after the Hindi word for a type of earthen pot it resembles. This structure forms around the external nasal openings and becomes fully developed when males reach sexual maturity at approximately 11 to 13 years of age. Males use the ghara to produce buzzing vocalizations and to generate visible bubble clusters on the water surface during courtship displays. It also serves as a visual signal of maturity and fitness. No other living crocodilian possesses a comparable nasal structure.

The body coloration is olive to dark grey-green dorsally, with lighter flanks and a yellowish or white underside. The legs are relatively weak and the feet are broadly webbed, reflecting the species' highly aquatic lifestyle. Unlike most other crocodilians, adult gharials are poorly equipped for terrestrial locomotion and spend the vast majority of their time in the water, hauling out primarily for basking and nesting.

## Distribution and Habitat

Historically, the gharial ranged across the major river systems of the northern Indian subcontinent, including the Indus, Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Mahanadi river basins, as well as the Irrawaddy River in Myanmar, encompassing portions of Pakistan, India, Nepal, Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Myanmar.

Today, the species' distribution has contracted dramatically. Breeding populations are confirmed in only a few river systems in India and Nepal. The most important remaining populations are found in the Chambal River (a tributary of the Yamuna in central India, harboring the largest single population), the Girwa River in the Katarniaghat Wildlife Sanctuary, and portions of the Son River system. In Nepal, small populations persist in the Rapti and Narayani rivers within Chitwan National Park.

Gharials are obligate riverine animals, inhabiting deep, clear, fast-flowing rivers with sandy banks suitable for basking and nesting. Unlike many other crocodilians, gharials avoid stagnant water bodies, marshes, and lakes. The species' strict habitat requirements make it particularly vulnerable to river damming and channelization, which alter flow regimes and fragment populations.

## Diet and Specialization

The gharial has one of the most specialized diets of any crocodilian. Adults feed almost exclusively on fish, making the species an obligate piscivore unmatched by other members of the order. The long, narrow snout offers minimal resistance as it sweeps through water, enabling rapid lateral strikes to capture fish. The sharp teeth interlock to create an effective trap from which slippery prey cannot escape. Juveniles also consume insects, crustaceans, and small frogs, but the fish-dominated diet is established early.

This extreme dietary specialization has ecological implications. Gharials play a role in regulating fish populations and may contribute to nutrient cycling by transporting nutrients to riverbanks through defecation at basking sites. However, the narrow diet makes the species vulnerable to declines in fish stocks caused by overfishing, pollution, and habitat degradation.

The gharial's reliance on fish has historically brought it into conflict with fishermen, who sometimes killed gharials in the belief that they competed for commercially valuable fish. In reality, gharials primarily consume species of limited commercial importance.

## Threats

The gharial has experienced one of the most severe population declines of any crocodilian. From an estimated 5,000 to 10,000 individuals in the 1940s, numbers plummeted to fewer than 200 breeding adults by the late 1990s. The total adult population is now estimated at fewer than 650 individuals, and the IUCN classifies the species as Critically Endangered.

The primary threats driving the gharial's decline are multifaceted. River damming and irrigation infrastructure have fragmented habitat and reduced the availability of deep pools essential for foraging. Sand mining on riverbanks destroys nesting sites and basking areas. Entanglement in fishing nets, both gillnets and seine nets, causes significant mortality, particularly among juveniles and sub-adults. Water pollution from industrial effluent, agricultural runoff, and untreated sewage degrades water quality and affects prey fish populations. Loss of nesting sites to agricultural encroachment along riverbanks further compounds the problem. The cumulative effect of these pressures has reduced the gharial to a small fraction of its former range.

## Conservation Efforts

Conservation programs for the gharial have been underway since the 1970s. India's gharial conservation program, initiated in 1975, established several breeding and rearing centers. The Kukrail Gharial Rehabilitation Centre near Lucknow in Uttar Pradesh became a major facility for captive breeding and head-starting, a strategy in which eggs are collected from wild nests, hatched in captivity, and juveniles are reared to a less vulnerable size before release into the wild. Thousands of gharials have been released through this program, though survival rates in degraded habitats remain a concern.

The Madras Crocodile Bank Trust, founded in 1976 by herpetologist Romulus Whitaker in Tamil Nadu, has played a significant role in crocodilian conservation, including maintaining a breeding population of gharials and conducting research on the species' ecology. In Nepal, gharial conservation is integrated into the management of Chitwan National Park.

Despite decades of effort, the gharial's long-term survival remains uncertain. Effective conservation requires continued captive breeding, large-scale protection of riverine habitats, regulation of sand mining and fishing practices, and reduction of water pollution.

## Cultural Significance and Taxonomy

The gharial holds a place in the cultural heritage of South Asia. In Hindu mythology, it is depicted as the vahana (vehicle) of the goddess Ganga, personification of the Ganges River. Representations of gharials appear in ancient Indian temples, coins, and sculptures, reflecting the species' historical abundance and cultural prominence.

The taxonomic placement of the gharial has been debated for decades. Morphological analyses traditionally place it in its own family, Gavialidae, separate from Crocodylidae and Alligatoridae. However, molecular phylogenetic studies have produced conflicting results, with some analyses suggesting that the false gharial (Tomistoma schlegelii) of Southeast Asia is more closely related to the gharial than to true crocodiles, which would require expanding Gavialidae to include Tomistoma. This unresolved question remains one of the most persistent phylogenetic controversies within Crocodilia.
