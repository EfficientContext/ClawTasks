# Green Sea Turtle (*Chelonia mydas*)

The green sea turtle is one of the largest and most widely distributed of the world's seven sea turtle species. Contrary to common assumption, its name derives not from the color of its shell or skin but from the green hue of its body fat, which was historically prized for the preparation of turtle soup. The green sea turtle is unique among marine turtles for its primarily herbivorous adult diet, a trait that gives it a central role in the ecology of seagrass meadows and makes it one of the few large marine herbivores remaining in the world's oceans.

## Description

Adult green sea turtles are large, robust animals. They typically reach a carapace length of 1.0 to 1.2 meters (3.3 to 3.9 feet), though exceptional individuals may exceed 1.5 meters. Adults weigh between 100 and 200 kilograms on average, with the largest recorded individuals approaching 300 kilograms (660 pounds). The carapace is smooth, oval, and relatively flat, coloured in shades of olive, brown, or dark grey, sometimes with radiating streaks or mottling. The plastron (underside) is pale yellow to white.

The head is small relative to body size and features a single pair of prefrontal scales, distinguishing it from the hawksbill, which has two pairs. The jaw is finely serrated, an adaptation for cropping seagrass and scraping algae from hard surfaces.

Two recognized subspecies exist: the Atlantic green turtle (*Chelonia mydas mydas*) and the Eastern Pacific green turtle (*Chelonia mydas agassizii*), the latter sometimes called the black sea turtle. Some authorities consider the Eastern Pacific population a separate species, but this remains debated.

## Herbivory and Seagrass Ecology

The green sea turtle is the only sea turtle species that is predominantly herbivorous as an adult. Juveniles are omnivorous, feeding on jellyfish, sponges, crustaceans, and other invertebrates in pelagic habitats. As they mature and recruit to nearshore environments, green turtles undergo a dietary shift and begin feeding almost exclusively on **seagrasses** and **marine algae**.

This herbivorous diet has profound ecological consequences. Green turtles are among the very few large-bodied grazers in marine ecosystems, and their feeding behavior acts as a form of ecosystem engineering. By regularly cropping seagrass blades, green turtles stimulate new growth and prevent seagrass beds from becoming overgrown and senescent. Grazed seagrass is typically more productive, more nutrient-rich, and more structurally diverse than ungrazed seagrass. The removal of older leaf tissue reduces the accumulation of epiphytes and detritus, improving light penetration and water flow within the meadow.

Healthy seagrass ecosystems provide critical services, including nursery habitat for commercially important fish species, sediment stabilization, and significant carbon sequestration. By maintaining seagrass health through grazing, green turtles contribute indirectly to these ecosystem functions. The historical decline of green turtle populations may have contributed to seagrass degradation in some regions.

## Distribution and Migration

Green sea turtles inhabit tropical and subtropical waters worldwide, typically in areas with abundant seagrass beds or algae-covered reefs. They are found throughout the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans, as well as the Mediterranean Sea. Important foraging areas include the Caribbean, the Great Barrier Reef, the Hawaiian Islands, the coasts of East Africa, and the waters surrounding the Galapagos Islands.

Green turtles undertake long-distance migrations between their foraging grounds and nesting beaches, with some individuals traveling over **2,600 kilometers** (1,600 miles) in a single migratory journey. Like other sea turtles, females exhibit natal homing, returning to the general region of their birth beach to nest. Males also migrate to the vicinity of nesting beaches to mate.

Major nesting aggregations occur at **Tortuguero, Costa Rica**, which hosts the largest green turtle rookery in the Atlantic; **Raine Island, Australia**, the largest nesting site in the world with tens of thousands of nesting females per season; and **Ascension Island** in the mid-Atlantic, where turtles migrate from feeding grounds along the coast of Brazil—a journey of approximately 2,300 kilometers across open ocean. Other significant nesting sites include the beaches of Oman, the Seychelles, Sabah (Malaysian Borneo), and the Hawaiian archipelago.

## Threats

Despite its wide distribution, the green sea turtle faces numerous threats. Historically, the species was heavily exploited for its meat, eggs, fat, and calipee (the cartilaginous tissue used in turtle soup). In many cultures, sea turtle eggs are considered a delicacy and are believed to have aphrodisiac properties. Although commercial harvest is now prohibited in most countries, illegal poaching of eggs and adults continues in parts of Southeast Asia, Central America, and the Western Indian Ocean.

**Bycatch** in commercial and artisanal fisheries remains a significant source of mortality. Green turtles are captured in trawl nets, gill nets, longlines, and pound nets, particularly in coastal waters where foraging habitat overlaps with fishing activity.

**Fibropapillomatosis** is a debilitating disease caused by a herpesvirus that produces tumor-like growths on the skin, eyes, and internal organs. The disease can impair vision, feeding, and swimming, and severe cases are fatal. It is particularly prevalent in Hawaii, Florida, and parts of Australia and Brazil, and may be linked to pollution and poor water quality.

**Habitat degradation** threatens both nesting and foraging environments. Coastal development and sand mining destroy nesting beaches. Seagrass meadows are declining globally due to pollution, dredging, and boat propeller scarring, directly reducing foraging habitat.

**Climate change** poses an escalating threat. Rising sand temperatures on nesting beaches are expected to skew sex ratios increasingly toward females due to temperature-dependent sex determination. Studies at Raine Island have already found that over 99 percent of juvenile green turtles originating from the warmer northern Great Barrier Reef nesting beaches are female. At extreme temperatures, hatching success declines and embryo mortality increases. Sea level rise may also inundate low-lying nesting beaches.

## Conservation Success

The green sea turtle is classified as **Endangered** on the IUCN Red List. However, the species represents one of the most encouraging conservation success stories among marine reptiles. At numerous nesting sites around the world, green turtle populations have shown significant recovery following decades of legal protection, beach monitoring, and anti-poaching enforcement.

At **Tortuguero**, where nesting was severely depleted by the mid-20th century, protection efforts beginning in the 1960s under the Caribbean Conservation Corporation (now the Sea Turtle Conservancy) have led to a dramatic increase in nesting numbers. Annual nest counts have risen from a few thousand in the 1970s to over 100,000 in recent years. Similarly, green turtle populations in **Hawaii** have increased substantially since the species was protected under the United States Endangered Species Act in 1978, with nesting at the French Frigate Shoals increasing by approximately 5 percent per year over several decades.

Recovery has also been documented at **Aldabra Atoll** in the Seychelles, on **Ascension Island**, and at sites in **Oman** and **Australia**. These recoveries demonstrate that green turtle populations can rebound when given sustained legal protection, habitat conservation, and effective enforcement against poaching.

International legal protections include listing under **CITES Appendix I**, the **Convention on Migratory Species**, and the **Inter-American Convention for the Protection and Conservation of Sea Turtles**. The ongoing recovery of the green sea turtle demonstrates that long-lived, slow-reproducing marine species can rebound from severe depletion when conservation action is sustained, but continued vigilance is necessary to address emerging threats including climate change, disease, and habitat loss.
