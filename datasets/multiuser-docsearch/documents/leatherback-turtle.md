# Leatherback Sea Turtle (*Dermochelys coriacea*)

The leatherback sea turtle is the largest living turtle and one of the most extraordinary reptiles on Earth. As the sole surviving member of the family Dermochelyidae, it is anatomically and physiologically distinct from the six hard-shelled species in the family Cheloniidae. Its unique adaptations—including a flexible, leathery carapace, the ability to dive to extreme depths, and a thermoregulatory system that allows it to inhabit frigid waters—make it one of the most remarkable products of more than 100 million years of marine turtle evolution.

## Description

Adult leatherback sea turtles are massive animals. The largest individuals can reach a curved carapace length of up to 2 meters (6.6 feet) and weigh as much as 700 kilograms (1,540 pounds), though typical adults range from 1.2 to 1.8 meters in length and 250 to 500 kilograms in weight. The leatherback's most distinctive feature is its carapace, which lacks the hard, keratinous scutes found on all other sea turtles. Instead, the shell is covered by a thick, rubbery skin reinforced by a mosaic of thousands of tiny dermal bones called osteoderms. Seven longitudinal ridges run along the length of the carapace, giving it a distinctive appearance and contributing to hydrodynamic efficiency.

The skin is predominantly dark grey or black, often with scattered white or pale blue spots. The front flippers are proportionally the longest of any sea turtle, spanning up to 2.7 meters from tip to tip, and lack the claws present in cheloniid species. The skull is adapted for a soft-bodied diet, with relatively weak jaws well suited for grasping slippery prey. The esophagus is lined with backward-pointing spines (papillae) that help retain gelatinous prey items during swallowing.

## Diving and Physiology

The leatherback sea turtle is the deepest-diving reptile ever recorded. Satellite-linked depth recorders have documented dives to 1,280 meters (4,200 feet), far exceeding the maximum known dive depths of any other reptile and rivaling those of many marine mammals. Typical foraging dives, however, are shallower, usually ranging from 50 to 300 meters, and last between 3 and 15 minutes. The leatherback's flexible, compressible carapace is thought to be a key adaptation for deep diving, as it can deform under pressure without cracking, unlike a rigid bony shell.

Several physiological adaptations support deep submersion. Leatherbacks possess high myoglobin concentrations in their muscles, enabling greater oxygen storage. They reduce their heart rate significantly during dives—a form of dive bradycardia that conserves oxygen. The lungs can collapse at depth, preventing nitrogen narcosis and decompression sickness by forcing gas out of the alveoli.

## Gigantothermy

Perhaps the most remarkable physiological trait of the leatherback is its ability to maintain a core body temperature substantially above that of the surrounding water—a phenomenon termed **gigantothermy** or inertial homeothermy. Unlike true endotherms such as mammals and birds, leatherbacks do not generate heat through a sustained high metabolic rate. Instead, they rely on a combination of three mechanisms: large body mass, peripheral insulation, and counter-current heat exchangers.

The leatherback's enormous size gives it a low surface-area-to-volume ratio, which slows heat loss to the environment. A thick layer of subcutaneous fat, unique among reptiles, provides insulation. Most critically, the blood vessels in the flippers are arranged in a **counter-current heat exchange** system: warm arterial blood flowing outward to the flippers passes adjacent to cool venous blood returning to the body core, transferring heat inward and minimizing thermal loss to the environment.

Together, these adaptations allow leatherbacks to maintain a body temperature 8 to 18 degrees Celsius above the ambient water temperature. This enables them to forage in waters as cold as 0 to 5 degrees Celsius, far beyond the thermal range tolerated by any other sea turtle. Leatherbacks have been recorded feeding in subpolar waters off the coasts of Norway, Newfoundland, and southern Chile, regions inhospitable to all other marine reptiles.

## Diet

Leatherback sea turtles feed primarily on **jellyfish** (Scyphozoa), as well as salps, pyrosomes, siphonophores, and ctenophores. Because jellyfish have low caloric density, leatherbacks must consume enormous quantities—an estimated 200 to 400 kilograms of prey per day during peak foraging. Their esophageal papillae prevent slippery prey from escaping.

Leatherbacks play a significant ecological role as top predators of jellyfish, and their decline may contribute to jellyfish population increases in some ocean basins.

## Migration

Leatherbacks are among the most migratory of all vertebrates. Satellite tracking studies have documented trans-oceanic journeys of extraordinary scope. Females nesting in Papua, Indonesia, have been tracked traveling over 12,000 kilometers across the Pacific Ocean to feeding grounds off the west coast of North America. In the Atlantic, individuals nesting in Trinidad, Gabon, and French Guiana migrate thousands of kilometers to temperate and subpolar foraging areas.

These migrations are driven by the seasonal availability of jellyfish aggregations, which tend to bloom in temperate and cold waters during spring and summer. Leatherbacks time their movements to exploit these ephemeral but productive food sources, following oceanographic features such as frontal zones and upwelling areas where prey concentrates.

## Nesting and Reproduction

Female leatherbacks nest on tropical sandy beaches, typically laying 4 to 7 clutches per nesting season at intervals of approximately 9 to 10 days. Each clutch contains roughly 80 to 100 fertilized eggs along with a variable number of smaller, yolkless spacer eggs whose function is debated but may aid gas exchange within the nest. The incubation period lasts about 60 to 70 days, and, as with other sea turtles, sex determination is temperature dependent.

Nesting females show strong fidelity to particular stretches of coastline but are somewhat less precise in their natal homing than some cheloniid species. Major nesting beaches include those in Trinidad and Tobago, Gabon, French Guiana and Suriname, and Papua in Indonesia.

## Conservation

The leatherback sea turtle is listed as **Vulnerable** on the IUCN Red List, but this global assessment masks dramatic regional differences. The **Eastern Pacific subpopulation** has experienced a catastrophic decline of more than 95 percent since the 1980s, driven primarily by bycatch in longline and gill net fisheries and by extensive egg harvesting on nesting beaches in Mexico and Central America. This subpopulation is considered Critically Endangered and faces a genuine risk of functional extinction.

The **Western Atlantic subpopulation**, by contrast, has shown encouraging signs of recovery in recent decades. Nesting numbers at key sites in Trinidad, French Guiana, and Suriname have increased following the implementation of conservation measures including beach patrols, nest protection programs, and fishing regulations. In the Northwest Atlantic, leatherback populations appear to be stable or increasing.

**Fisheries bycatch** remains the single greatest threat to leatherback populations globally. Longlines set for tuna and swordfish, as well as coastal gill nets, capture and drown significant numbers of leatherbacks each year. The ingestion of **plastic debris**, particularly plastic bags that resemble jellyfish in the water column, is another major cause of mortality. Necropsies of stranded leatherbacks frequently reveal large quantities of plastic in the gastrointestinal tract.

Conservation strategies include international agreements to reduce bycatch, mandatory use of circle hooks in longline fisheries, protection of nesting beaches, and public education campaigns to reduce plastic pollution. The species' vast migratory range, crossing numerous national jurisdictions and the high seas, makes international cooperation essential for its survival.
