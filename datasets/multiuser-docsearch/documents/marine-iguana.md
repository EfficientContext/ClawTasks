# Marine Iguana (*Amblyrhynchus cristatus*)

The marine iguana is the world's only ocean-going lizard and one of the most iconic animals of the Galapagos Islands. Endemic to this volcanic archipelago in the eastern Pacific Ocean, it has evolved a suite of extraordinary adaptations for foraging on marine algae in cold, turbulent waters—a lifestyle unique among living reptiles. First described to the Western world by Charles Darwin during the voyage of HMS Beagle in 1835, the marine iguana has since become a symbol of the remarkable evolutionary processes that occur on isolated oceanic islands.

## Description and Evolution

Marine iguanas are stocky, dark-colored lizards with laterally flattened tails, blunt snouts, and long, curved claws. Adults range considerably in size depending on the island population, with males reaching total lengths of up to 1.3 meters and weights of 12 kilograms, while females are substantially smaller, sometimes weighing as little as 1 kilogram. The overall coloration is dark grey to black, aiding absorption of solar radiation during basking. During the breeding season, males develop striking patches of bright red, green, and turquoise, the hues varying by island.

The genus *Amblyrhynchus* diverged from the Galapagos land iguana (*Conolophus*) approximately **4.5 million years ago**, based on molecular phylogenetic estimates. The ancestor likely arrived by rafting on vegetation from South America. Once established, the lineage adapted to exploit abundant marine algae on intertidal and subtidal rocks—a resource with no terrestrial competitors.

At least eleven subspecies are currently recognized across the archipelago, differing in body size, coloration, and scale morphology. Genetic studies have revealed significant divergence among island populations, reflecting the isolating effect of deep-water channels.

## Marine Foraging

The marine iguana's most distinctive behavior is its aquatic foraging. Iguanas feed primarily on **red algae** (*Rhodophyta*) and **green algae** (*Chlorophyta*) growing on rocks in the intertidal and subtidal zones. Smaller individuals forage in the intertidal zone at low tide, scraping algae with their blunt, tricuspid teeth. Larger individuals, particularly adult males, dive beneath the surface to graze on submerged algal beds.

Diving iguanas have been recorded at depths of up to **12 meters** (39 feet), though most dives are between 1 and 5 meters. They can hold their breath for **30 minutes or more**, though typical dives last 5 to 15 minutes. The heart rate slows dramatically during submersion—a form of dive bradycardia—conserving oxygen. The laterally compressed tail propels the iguana with undulating movements, while the long claws grip rocky substrates against strong currents.

Foraging bouts are brief, usually less than an hour, because cold water rapidly lowers body temperature and impairs muscle function. After foraging, iguanas must return to shore to rewarm.

## Salt Excretion

A major physiological challenge of feeding on marine algae is the ingestion of large quantities of seawater and salt. Marine iguanas have evolved highly efficient **nasal salt glands** that excrete a concentrated solution of sodium chloride and potassium chloride. These glands are located in the nasal cavity and are connected to the nostrils by a series of ducts.

The accumulated salt is periodically expelled in a forceful nasal exhalation—the famous **"sneezing"** behavior frequently observed in basking iguanas. The expelled salt often crystallizes on the head, giving a white, encrusted appearance. This system is functionally analogous to the salt glands of seabirds and sea turtles but evolved independently. The glands can produce a secretion with a salt concentration roughly twice that of seawater, maintaining osmotic balance despite the marine diet.

## Thermoregulation

As an ectothermic reptile foraging in the cold waters of the **Humboldt Current**, which bathes the Galapagos with temperatures often between 15 and 25 degrees Celsius (and occasionally lower), the marine iguana faces severe thermoregulatory challenges. Water conducts heat away from the body approximately 25 times faster than air, and iguanas can lose several degrees of core body temperature during a single foraging dive.

To compensate, marine iguanas spend much of their day **basking** on dark lava rocks, which absorb and radiate solar heat efficiently. Their dark skin coloration maximizes the absorption of solar radiation. Iguanas typically orient themselves perpendicular to the sun's rays to maximize the body surface area exposed to sunlight and may stack on top of one another in dense aggregations to reduce heat loss.

Marine iguanas also control heat exchange physiologically. During basking, peripheral blood vessels dilate to accelerate warming; during swimming, vasoconstriction slows heat loss. The rate of heat gain during basking is approximately twice the rate of heat loss during swimming, indicating active modulation rather than passive thermal exchange.

## Body Size Variation and El Nino Vulnerability

Body size varies dramatically among island populations, with larger individuals on islands with more productive algal beds. The marine iguana is exceptionally vulnerable to **El Nino-Southern Oscillation (ENSO)** events. During El Nino years, warm water replaces nutrient-rich cold upwelling, causing preferred algae to die off and be replaced by indigestible brown algae. The resulting food shortage triggers mass starvation, with mortality exceeding **90 percent** in some populations.

Remarkably, research has demonstrated that marine iguanas can actually **shrink in body length** during periods of food scarcity—a phenomenon extremely rare among vertebrates. Individuals have been documented to decrease in snout-to-vent length by as much as 20 percent, apparently through the resorption of bone tissue. This reduction in body size lowers metabolic demands and may improve survival during famine conditions. When food becomes available again, iguanas regrow to their former size.

## Darwin's Observations

Charles Darwin encountered marine iguanas during his visit to the Galapagos in 1835. He described them as "hideous-looking" and "most disgusting, clumsy lizards" in *The Voyage of the Beagle*, but was fascinated by their marine habits. Darwin conducted experiments, repeatedly throwing iguanas into the water and observing that they invariably swam back to shore, which he attributed to their fear of land-based predators rather than ocean threats.

Darwin's observations of the marine iguana, alongside his study of the Galapagos finches and tortoises, contributed to his theory of evolution by natural selection, illustrating how species adapt to exploit novel ecological niches on isolated islands.

## Conservation

The marine iguana is classified as **Vulnerable** on the IUCN Red List, with several island subspecies considered Endangered or Critically Endangered. The primary threats are **introduced predators**, including feral cats, rats, and dogs, which prey on eggs, juveniles, and occasionally adults. Cats and rats are particularly devastating to iguana populations on islands where these predators have become established, as the iguanas evolved in the absence of terrestrial mammalian predators and have limited anti-predator defenses against them.

Other threats include oil spills (the 2001 grounding of the tanker *Jessica* near San Cristobal Island caused documented mortality), disturbance from tourism, potential disease transmission from domestic animals, and the increasing frequency and intensity of El Nino events associated with climate change.

Conservation measures include **eradication of invasive species** from key islands by the Galapagos National Park Service and the Charles Darwin Foundation. Successful rat and cat removal programs have led to measurable improvements in iguana populations. Strict tourism regulations, including designated visitor trails and minimum approach distances, minimize human disturbance, and long-term monitoring programs provide data for adaptive management.
