# Global Reptile Conservation

Reptiles represent one of the most diverse yet historically neglected groups of vertebrates in terms of conservation attention. With over 12,000 described species spanning turtles, crocodilians, lizards, snakes, amphisbaenians, and the tuatara, reptiles occupy virtually every terrestrial and many aquatic ecosystems on Earth. Despite their ecological importance as predators, prey, seed dispersers, and ecosystem engineers, reptiles have long received less conservation funding and research effort than mammals and birds. The first comprehensive global assessment of reptile extinction risk, published in Nature in 2022, revealed that the conservation status of reptiles is more precarious than many had assumed.

## Threat Assessment

The 2022 global assessment, conducted by an international team of over 900 scientists under the auspices of the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), evaluated the extinction risk of 10,196 reptile species. The results demonstrated that at least 21.1% of assessed reptile species are threatened with extinction, classified as Vulnerable, Endangered, or Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. This proportion is broadly comparable to the figures for mammals (approximately 25%) and birds (approximately 13%), confirming that reptiles face extinction pressures of similar magnitude to other well-studied vertebrate groups.

Turtles and crocodilians emerged as disproportionately threatened, with over 50% of turtle and tortoise species and approximately 50% of crocodilian species classified as threatened. Squamates (lizards and snakes), while less threatened as a group percentage-wise, account for the largest absolute number of threatened reptile species owing to their extraordinary diversity.

## Major Threats

Habitat loss and degradation constitute the single greatest threat to reptile biodiversity worldwide. Agricultural expansion, urbanization, logging, and infrastructure development destroy and fragment the habitats upon which reptiles depend. This is particularly acute in tropical regions, where reptile diversity is highest and deforestation rates remain elevated. Forest-dependent species, including many chameleons, geckos, and arboreal snakes, are especially vulnerable to habitat conversion.

Overexploitation represents the second major threat category. Reptiles are harvested globally for food, traditional medicine, leather, and the international pet trade. Sea turtles have been hunted for centuries for their meat, eggs, and shells. Freshwater turtles are collected in enormous numbers for consumption in East and Southeast Asian markets. The exotic pet trade drives collection of rare and range-restricted species, with some species commanding prices of thousands of dollars per individual, creating powerful incentives for illegal harvest. The gharial (Gavialis gangeticus), ploughshare tortoise (Astrochelys yniphora), and Roti Island snake-necked turtle (Chelodina mccordi) are among the most critically endangered reptile species on Earth, all suffering from a combination of habitat loss and direct exploitation.

Invasive species pose severe threats, particularly on islands. Introduced rats, cats, mongooses, and pigs prey on reptile eggs and juveniles, while invasive reptiles themselves can outcompete or prey upon native species. The brown tree snake (Boiga irregularis) devastated the native reptile and bird fauna of Guam following its accidental introduction after World War II.

Pollution, including pesticide contamination, plastic ingestion by marine turtles, and heavy metal accumulation in crocodilians, adds further pressure. Emerging infectious diseases, including fungal pathogens, represent a growing concern, though their population-level impacts on reptiles remain less documented than in amphibians.

## CITES and Trade

The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES) is the primary international mechanism regulating the trade of reptile species across national borders. CITES operates through a system of appendices. Appendix I includes species threatened with extinction for which commercial international trade is generally prohibited, such as all sea turtle species, the gharial, and numerous tortoise species. Appendix II includes species not necessarily threatened with extinction but for which trade must be controlled to prevent unsustainable exploitation; this includes many python, monitor, and chameleon species. Appendix III includes species protected in at least one country that has requested CITES assistance in controlling trade.

Enforcement remains a persistent challenge. Illegal wildlife trade is estimated to be worth billions of dollars annually, and reptiles constitute a significant fraction. Smuggling of rare tortoises, geckos, and venomous snakes continues despite CITES protections, driven by demand from collectors and traditional medicine markets.

## Conservation Successes

Despite the grim overall picture, several notable conservation successes demonstrate that recovery is possible with sustained effort. The American alligator (Alligator mississippiensis) is perhaps the most celebrated reptile conservation success story. Listed as endangered under the U.S. Endangered Species Act in 1967, the species recovered dramatically following hunting restrictions, habitat protection, and regulated farming programs. It was reclassified as recovered in 1987 and now supports regulated harvest across much of the southeastern United States.

The Aldabra giant tortoise (Aldabrachelys gigantea) persists in large numbers on the Aldabra Atoll in the Seychelles, thanks to the atoll's remote location and protected status as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Conservation management has maintained a population of approximately 100,000 individuals, making it one of the largest giant tortoise populations in the world.

Several sea turtle populations have shown encouraging trends. Nesting populations of green sea turtles (Chelonia mydas) have increased at multiple monitored beaches following decades of protection, nest monitoring, and reduction in bycatch through turtle excluder devices (TEDs) in shrimp trawl fisheries.

## Climate Change

Climate change poses uniquely complex threats to reptile conservation. For species with temperature-dependent sex determination (TSD), which includes all crocodilians, most turtles, and some lizards, rising incubation temperatures can produce highly skewed sex ratios. In sea turtles, warmer sand temperatures are already producing clutches that are over 90% female at some nesting beaches, raising concerns about future population viability if warming continues unabated.

Sea level rise threatens low-lying nesting beaches used by sea turtles and some crocodilians. Storm surge flooding can destroy nests, and permanent inundation may eliminate traditional nesting habitat entirely. Range contractions are predicted for many reptile species, particularly those in montane habitats and on small islands, where upslope or poleward range shifts are geographically constrained.

Altered precipitation patterns affect reptile habitats indirectly by changing vegetation structure and prey availability. Prolonged droughts can reduce water availability for freshwater turtles and crocodilians, while changes in fire regimes alter habitat suitability for many terrestrial species.

## Priority Actions

Effective reptile conservation requires action across multiple fronts. Habitat protection remains paramount, with the expansion and effective management of protected areas in biodiversity-rich tropical regions as a top priority. The IUCN Species Survival Commission's Crocodile Specialist Group coordinates conservation actions for crocodilians worldwide, while the Turtle Survival Alliance focuses on the most critically endangered chelonian species through captive breeding, habitat protection, and community engagement.

Island Conservation, an international nonprofit, works to remove invasive species from islands, directly benefiting endemic reptile populations. Captive breeding programs have played important roles for some critically endangered species, such as the ploughshare tortoise and the Antiguan racer (Alsophis antiguae), though reintroduction success varies and captive breeding is generally viewed as a complement to, not a substitute for, in-situ habitat conservation.

Reducing road mortality through wildlife crossings and road ecology research is an emerging priority, as roads represent a significant and growing source of reptile mortality worldwide. Addressing the illegal pet trade through improved enforcement, demand reduction campaigns, and support for legal and sustainable alternatives is also critical. Ultimately, integrating reptile conservation into broader landscape-level planning and climate adaptation strategies will be essential for safeguarding this group of vertebrates into the future.
