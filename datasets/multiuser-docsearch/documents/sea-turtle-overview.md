# Sea Turtles: An Overview

Sea turtles are among the most ancient reptiles inhabiting the world's oceans. Having survived for more than 110 million years, these marine chelonians have witnessed the rise and fall of the dinosaurs and have remained relatively unchanged in body plan since the Cretaceous period. Today, seven living species persist across all oceans except the Arctic, yet nearly all face serious threats from human activity and environmental change.

## Diversity and Classification

The seven extant species of sea turtle are divided into two families. The family **Dermochelyidae** contains a single living species, the leatherback sea turtle (*Dermochelys coriacea*), which is distinguished by its lack of a hard, bony shell. The remaining six species belong to the family **Cheloniidae**: the green sea turtle (*Chelonia mydas*), loggerhead sea turtle (*Caretta caretta*), hawksbill sea turtle (*Eretmochelys imbricata*), Kemp's ridley sea turtle (*Lepidochelys kempii*), olive ridley sea turtle (*Lepidochelys olivacea*), and flatback sea turtle (*Natator depressus*).

These species vary considerably in size, diet, and habitat preference. The leatherback is the largest, reaching lengths of over two meters and weights exceeding 700 kilograms, while the two ridley species are the smallest, typically weighing between 30 and 50 kilograms. The hawksbill is noted for its narrow, pointed beak adapted to extracting sponges from coral crevices, and the green sea turtle is unique among the group as the only primarily herbivorous species in adulthood.

The evolutionary history of sea turtles stretches back to the late Mesozoic Era. Fossil evidence indicates that the basic sea turtle body plan—a streamlined shell, flipper-like limbs, and salt-excreting glands—was already well established by the Late Cretaceous. Ancient relatives such as *Archelon*, which lived approximately 75 million years ago, reached lengths of over four meters.

## General Morphology

Sea turtles share a suite of anatomical adaptations for life in the ocean. Their shells are more streamlined and hydrodynamic than those of terrestrial turtles, reducing drag during swimming. The forelimbs have evolved into large, powerful flippers that provide thrust, while the shorter hind limbs serve as rudders for steering. Unlike freshwater turtles, sea turtles cannot retract their heads or limbs into their shells.

A critical physiological adaptation is the presence of salt glands, located near the eyes, which allow sea turtles to excrete excess salt absorbed from seawater. These glands produce a highly concentrated saline solution, giving the appearance of "crying" when the turtles are on land. Sea turtles also possess excellent underwater vision and are believed to perceive ultraviolet wavelengths.

## Migration

Sea turtles are renowned for undertaking some of the longest migrations in the animal kingdom. Many species travel thousands of kilometers between their feeding grounds and nesting beaches. Leatherback turtles have been tracked crossing entire ocean basins, with individuals migrating from nesting beaches in Indonesia to feeding areas off the coast of California, a journey spanning more than 10,000 kilometers.

One of the most remarkable aspects of sea turtle migration is natal homing—the tendency of females to return to the beach where they hatched to lay their own eggs, sometimes decades later. Research has demonstrated that sea turtles navigate using the Earth's magnetic field, detecting both the intensity and inclination angle of geomagnetic lines to determine their position. This magnetic sense may be supplemented by visual cues, such as the position of the sun, and chemical cues, including the unique scent signature of their natal beach carried by ocean currents.

## Nesting

All sea turtle species are oviparous, and females must come ashore to lay their eggs on sandy beaches. The nesting process follows a highly conserved behavioral sequence: the female crawls above the high-tide line, excavates a body pit with her front flippers, digs an egg chamber with her rear flippers, deposits a clutch of eggs (typically 50 to 200 depending on species), covers the nest with sand, and returns to the sea. The entire process may take one to three hours.

Some species, particularly the olive ridley and Kemp's ridley, engage in mass nesting events known as **arribadas**, during which thousands of females converge on a single beach over several days. The beaches of Ostional in Costa Rica and Gahirmatha in India are famous for hosting these aggregations, which can involve hundreds of thousands of individuals.

Sea turtle eggs incubate for approximately 45 to 70 days, depending on species and environmental conditions. Crucially, the sex of the developing embryo is determined not by chromosomes but by the temperature of the nest during the middle third of incubation—a phenomenon known as **temperature-dependent sex determination (TSD)**. Warmer temperatures generally produce females, while cooler temperatures produce males. The pivotal temperature, at which a roughly equal sex ratio is produced, is approximately 29 degrees Celsius for most species.

## Threats

Sea turtles face a multitude of threats, many of which are anthropogenic in origin. **Bycatch** in commercial fisheries—particularly in trawl nets, longlines, and gill nets—is among the leading causes of sea turtle mortality worldwide. Turtles become entangled in fishing gear and drown, as they must surface periodically to breathe.

**Plastic pollution** poses a growing danger. Sea turtles, especially leatherbacks, frequently ingest plastic debris such as bags and film, which they mistake for jellyfish or other prey. Ingested plastic can cause intestinal blockages, malnutrition, and death. Entanglement in discarded fishing line and nets is also common.

**Coastal development** threatens nesting habitat by destroying or degrading sandy beaches. Construction, sand mining, and the placement of seawalls and other hard structures can render beaches unsuitable for nesting. Artificial **light pollution** from beachfront buildings disorients hatchlings, which rely on the natural light of the ocean horizon to navigate from nest to sea; misdirected hatchlings that wander inland are exposed to predation, dehydration, and vehicle strikes.

**Egg harvesting** and the hunting of adult turtles for meat, shells, and traditional medicine persist in many regions. **Climate change** compounds these threats by raising sand temperatures, potentially skewing sex ratios heavily toward females and reducing hatching success. Rising sea levels also threaten to inundate low-lying nesting beaches.

## Conservation

International and national efforts to protect sea turtles have expanded significantly over the past half-century. All seven species are listed under the **Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES)**, which prohibits international commercial trade in sea turtle products. Most species are classified as Endangered or Vulnerable on the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List.

A landmark innovation has been the development and mandated use of **Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs)** in shrimp trawl nets. TEDs are grated trapdoors that allow captured turtles to escape while retaining shrimp, and their implementation has significantly reduced bycatch mortality.

**Marine protected areas (MPAs)** safeguard critical foraging and nesting habitats. Notable examples include the Tortuguero National Park in Costa Rica, which protects one of the Western Hemisphere's most important green turtle nesting beaches, and the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park in Australia, which encompasses key habitats for multiple species.

Community-based **beach monitoring programs** employ local residents and volunteers to patrol nesting beaches, protect nests from poachers and predators, relocate nests threatened by erosion or flooding, and collect data on nesting activity. These programs have contributed to population recoveries in several regions, demonstrating that sustained conservation action can reverse decades of decline.
